I. Apparatus in Full Elevation
Side view · scale 1:20 · all dimensions in centimetres
The apparatus stands upon a welded-steel tripod from which rises the central pole bearing three independently-rotating disks. Each disk carries its own motor and slip-ring assembly at the hub, permitting continuous rotation without conductor entanglement.
II. Geometria Discorum — Plan Views
Plan view from above · arm patterns, LED positions, moiré relationship
Three independently-driven disks, each offset in rotational phase. Contra-rotation at speeds differing by ≈7% generates moiré interference with apparent fractal dimension D≈1.6 — within the human visual preference optimum of D=1.4–1.6. Effective photic frequency equals (RPM ÷ 60) × arm count.
III. Annulus Electricus — Slip Ring Cross-Section
Section A–A' · scale 1:2 · 6-conductor gold-contact assembly
Six independent circuits traverse the rotating joint — power rails for motor and LED logic, the serial LED data signal, and I²C for the onboard sensors. Gold contacts ensure minimal resistance across continuous rotation; the sealed ball bearing above receives the mechanical axial load.
IV. Machina Motrix — Drive Train Assembly
Exploded elevation · geared DC motor, encoder & disk hub
Each of three disks has its own geared DC motor (ratio 1:60), permitting independent speed control from 1–30 RPM via PWM through an L298N dual H-bridge. A 20-slot encoder disk with infrared sensor provides tachometer feedback to the ESP32 closed-loop PID controller.
V. Nexus Intelligens — Control Architecture
System topology · ESP32 · power rails · data buses · WiFi
The ESP32 orchestrates all subsystems under FreeRTOS: independent PID loops for each disk motor, LED animation via FastLED, strobe timing (hardware-interrupt hard-capped at 4 Hz), DMX spot control, audio synchronisation, and a WiFi access point serving the real-time JSON preset API for DJ/VJ integration.
VI. Homo in Machina — Experience Geometry & Human Scale
Lateral section · participant position, visual field, projected light cone, acoustic field
The participant stands approximately 100 cm before the apparatus, positioning Disk 2 at eye level. The projected light field subtends ≈45° of visual arc — sufficient to engage peripheral entrainment pathways in the primary visual cortex. Three independent motor speeds allow simultaneous targeting of theta (4–8 Hz), alpha (8–12 Hz), and gamma (40 Hz invisible-spectral) bands across the disk stack.
On the nature of effective frequency: a disk bearing four arms spinning at twelve revolutions per minute shall produce eight pulses of light per second upon the retina — identical in neural effect to a strobe set to eight hertz, yet without the strobe's abrupt violence. The geometry is the mechanism; the rotation is the clock.
On moiré and complexity: two overlapping patterns of differing spatial frequency combine to produce an interference pattern whose fractal dimension exceeds that of either constituent. Counter-rotation at 7% speed differential generates emergent D≈1.6, the optimum of human visual preference according to Taylor et al. Neither disk alone exceeds the aversion threshold of D=1.7.
Safety principle, paramount above all: the photosensitive epilepsy threshold commences at three hertz, reaching peak danger near fifteen. The apparatus is constrained by hardware interrupt — not software, which may fail — to produce no effective frequency above four hertz. This limit is inviolable in firmware and physically documented on the apparatus panel.
