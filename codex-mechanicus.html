I. Façade — Three Disks Facing the Observer
Front elevation · observer's view · disks rotate in their own vertical planes · scale 1:5
The observer faces three vertically-standing disks in a column. Each disk rotates in its own plane — like a clock face — with alternating clockwise and counter-clockwise direction. From this vantage the full LED geometry, arm symmetry, and moiré interference between overlapping disks are all directly visible.
II. Apparatus Profile — Side Elevation
View from right · disks edge-on · motor units behind each disk · heights to ground
In profile the disks appear as thin vertical lines — they stand perpendicular to the ground, face-on to the observer. Each disk sits on its own horizontal axle driven by a motor/gearbox unit attached to the rear face of the mounting column. An independent slip ring at the back of each unit feeds power and data to the rotating LEDs.
III. Discus Magnus — Disk Face Detail · Two Material Variants
Front face · ∅ 60 cm · 4-arm · Option A: skeletal frame · Option B: acrylic-infilled
Option A (skeletal): aluminium alloy arms with SK6812 strips in routed channels. Moiré arises entirely from persistence-of-vision LED trails — cleanest for neural entrainment. Option B (acrylic): 6mm cast acrylic panels fill the sectors between arms, allowing edge-lit glow and optional laser-etched patterns. Both mount identically on the same hub and axle.
IV. Sectio Axialis — Motor, Axle & Slip Ring
Horizontal cross-section through one disk unit · front (observer side) left
From the observer's side leftward: disk face → hub plate → front bearing → axle → gearbox → motor body → encoder wheel → slip ring → frame mount. The slip ring at the rear of each unit feeds six gold-contact circuits (GND, +5 V, +12 V, LED DATA, SDA, SCL) from the fixed frame wiring to the rotating axle and disk.
V. Nexus Intelligens — Control Architecture
ESP32 system topology · 3× motor PID · LED · Strobe · DMX · WiFi preset API
ESP32 orchestrates all subsystems under FreeRTOS: three independent motor PID loops, LED animation via FastLED through slip rings, strobe timing via hardware interrupt (hard-capped at 4 Hz), DMX spot lights, audio sync, and a WiFi access point serving the real-time JSON preset API for DJ/VJ scripting.
VI. Homo in Machina — Experience Geometry & Human Scale
Front-lateral view · participant faces the column of three vertical disk faces · visual field, light cones, acoustic field
The participant stands roughly 100 cm in front of the column, with Disk 2 at eye level. Each disk face rotates in the vertical plane, visible directly. Three motors run at independent speeds, allowing simultaneous multi-band photic entrainment across the disk stack. The light cone of Disk 1 additionally reaches the observer's peripheral vision, widening the entrainment field beyond the central 45° arc.
On disk orientation: the disks stand vertical in the plane of the observer's face — each spinning like a clock, not like a record player. The observer does not look down at the mechanism but directly into it, as one looks into the face of a wheel or a window. This is the correct geometry for photic entrainment: the rotating arm sweeps across the retina at the computed effective frequency.
On the skeletal versus infilled disk: the skeletal arm frame produces the sharper entrainment geometry, its LED points tracing precise arcs across the visual field. The acrylic-infilled variant adds spectacle — the panels glow from within, and the moiré is visible as a physical interference between rotating etched surfaces. A festival demands both; perhaps the choice varies by preset.
On safety: the effective frequency formula applies to disk rotation, not to the strobe. The strobe is separately constrained by hardware interrupt to ≤ 4 Hz absolute, regardless of software state. At 12 RPM with 4 arms, effective frequency is 0.8 Hz — far below the photosensitive epilepsy danger zone of 3–30 Hz. Higher disk speeds with fewer arms keep the same safety margin.
